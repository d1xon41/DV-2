Global Context

Australia in the World

By Dixon Salim — Data: CAPAD 2024, DCCEEW

Megadiversity Nation

Australia is one of only 17 megadiverse countries on Earth — a group that together holds over 70% of the world's biodiversity.

70% of world's biodiversity in just 17 nations

Its geographic isolation over millions of years produced species found nowhere else: kangaroos, koalas, platypuses, and thousands of unique plant species.

A Conservation Giant

Today, Australia's national parks and protected areas form the backbone of global conservation efforts in the Southern Hemisphere. The National Reserve System spans over 173 million hectares.

Australia's protected area network is one of the largest on Earth by total land area — yet biodiversity loss continues at an alarming rate.

The Challenge Ahead

Despite this scale, the continent faces a deepening crisis. The same isolation that created unparalleled endemism also left its species uniquely unprepared for introduced predators, land clearing, and climate disruption.

"A nation of superlatives — in wildlife, in wilderness, and in loss."

The Protection Gap

Protection levels vary dramatically across Australia's states and territories. The Australian Capital Territory leads with over 55% of its land formally protected — partly by design as a capital territory with nature reserves built in from the start.

10.4% NSW — most biodiverse, least protected

Queensland and New South Wales protect less than 10% of their land despite hosting the continent's most biodiverse ecosystems including the Wet Tropics and the Great Barrier Reef hinterland.

Distribution

Where Are Parks Concentrated?

Victoria vs Western Australia

The number of protected areas and the percentage of land protected tell very different stories. Victoria has nearly 5,000 individual protected areas — more than any other state.

4,967 protected areas in Victoria alone

But only 18% of its land is formally protected, reflecting a network of small, fragmented reserves.

The Desert Paradox

Western Australia, by contrast, has far fewer individual parks but protects over 31% of its vast land area through large desert reserves.

The largest single protected areas in Australia are in WA — vast desert reserves with little agricultural value but immense ecological significance.

Over Time

How Protection Has Grown Since 2000

Two Decades of Change

Between 2000 and 2020, Australia added over 40 million hectares to its National Reserve System — a remarkable expansion driven by federal funding programs and Indigenous Protected Area declarations.

+40M hectares added to NRS since 2000

The animated map below shows how protection has spread across each state over time. Drag the year slider to replay the growth story.

SA grew its protected percentage more than any other state, from under 20% in 2000 to over 28% by 2020 — largely through the Great Western Woodlands and desert conservation initiatives.

Despite these gains, Australia still needs to add approximately 60 million hectares to meet the "30 by 30" global biodiversity target it has signed onto.

What IUCN Category Tells Us

The International Union for Conservation of Nature classifies protected areas from Ia (strict nature reserve) to VI (sustainable use). Most of Australia's park area falls in Category VI — areas that permit sustainable resource use.

Cat. VI dominant by land area in Australia

Strict protection (Category Ia/Ib) is rare but scientifically vital as it provides undisturbed baseline ecosystems.

The Governance Mosaic

Australia's national parks are managed by a patchwork of institutions. Government agencies manage the majority in number, but community and joint management — including Indigenous co-management — covers the largest land area by contribution to the National Reserve System.

7,299 parks managed by government agencies

Yet just 83 community-governed areas cover more total land than all of government's 7,299 — revealing the sheer scale of individual Indigenous Protected Areas.

Bioregions & Visitors

Which Bioregions Are Best Protected?

89 Distinct Worlds

Australia is divided into 89 bioregions under the IBRA framework — each a distinct ecological zone defined by geology, climate, flora and fauna.

"The most biodiverse parts of Australia are often the least protected — while the most protected regions are largely arid desert."

Arid desert bioregions in WA and the NT are well represented, often because they have little agricultural value. But biodiversity-rich regions — tropical savannas, temperate woodlands, coastal heaths — remain badly underrepresented.

Extinction Crisis

Australia's Record of Species Loss

34 Mammal species extinct
#1 Worst mammal extinction rate on Earth
2019 First climate extinction recorded
1900s Peak extinction decade

The Worst on Earth

Australia has the worst mammal extinction record of any country on Earth. Since European settlement in 1788, at least 34 mammal species have been declared extinct — more than any other nation.

The Bramble Cay Melomys, a small rodent on a single island in the Torres Strait, was declared extinct in 2019 — the world's first recorded mammal extinction caused directly by climate change.

The causes are well understood: land clearing destroyed habitat at industrial scale, introduced predators such as foxes and cats decimated small native mammals, and altered fire regimes transformed entire ecosystems.

The Full Picture

Australia's Protected Areas at a Glance

The National Reserve System

The National Reserve System is the culmination of decades of conservation effort by governments, Indigenous communities, scientists, and private landholders. It is one of the largest protected area networks on Earth by total land area.

30% protected land target by 2030

Yet the work is far from finished. Scientists estimate that at least 30% of Australia's land area needs formal protection to halt biodiversity decline — the "30 by 30" target that Australia has committed to achieving by 2030.

The Race to 30 by 30

With 22.6% currently protected, the gap is real but closeable. Every park declared, every co-management agreement signed, every private covenant registered brings Australia closer to that goal.

"Australia has committed to 30% protected land by 2030. With 22.6% today, the race is on."— 30x30 Target, Kunming-Montreal Agreement

The annotated map at right shows each state's current protection level alongside key ecological landmarks and the scale of the challenge ahead.

What Must Change

Meeting the 30 by 30 target requires prioritising the regions with the biggest biodiversity shortfall — not just the places where protection is politically easy or economically cheap.

NSW and QLD together account for over 60% of Australia's endemic species yet protect less than 10% of their land. Bridging this gap is the single most important conservation action available.
