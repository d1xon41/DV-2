00 — GLOBAL CONTEXT

Australia in the world

Australia is one of only 17 megadiverse countries on Earth — a group that together holds over 70% of the world's biodiversity. Its geographic isolation over millions of years produced species found nowhere else: kangaroos, koalas, platypuses, and thousands of unique plant species. Today, Australia's national parks and protected areas form the backbone of global conservation efforts in the Southern Hemisphere. 01 — COVERAGE

How much of each state is protected?

Protection levels vary dramatically across Australia's states and territories. The Australian Capital Territory leads with over 55% of its land formally protected — partly because it was designed as a capital territory with large nature reserves built in from the start. At the other extreme, Queensland and New South Wales protect less than 10% of their land despite hosting the continent's most biodiverse ecosystems including the Wet Tropics and the Great Barrier Reef hinterland. 01B — DISTRIBUTION

Where are parks concentrated?

The number of protected areas and the percentage of land protected tell very different stories. Victoria has nearly 5,000 individual protected areas — more than any other state — but only 18% of its land is formally protected, reflecting a network of small, fragmented reserves. Western Australia, by contrast, has far fewer individual parks but protects over 31% of its vast land area through large desert reserves. 01C — COUNT

Number of parks per state

When we look at raw park counts rather than percentage protected, the spatial pattern shifts dramatically. Victoria dominates not because it is the most committed to conservation, but because it has historically created thousands of small nature reserves, flora reserves, and conservation parks spread across the landscape. This fragmentation is itself a conservation concern — small isolated reserves support fewer species than large connected ones.

01D — OVER TIME

How has protection grown over time?

Australia's commitment to conservation has grown steadily since 2000. The federal government's National Reserve System targets protecting a comprehensive, adequate and representative sample of Australia's biodiversity. Between 2000 and 2020, total protected land grew from around 10% to over 22% of the continent. South Australia and Western Australia drove much of this growth through the declaration of large Indigenous Protected Areas — a recognition that Indigenous land management has protected biodiversity for tens of thousands of years. 02 — QUANTITY

What does the scale of protection look like?

Size matters enormously in conservation. A single large reserve is far more ecologically valuable than many small, disconnected ones because it supports viable populations of wide-ranging animals, maintains intact food webs, and allows species to move as the climate changes. 03 — CLASSIFICATION

How are protected areas classified?

Not all protected areas offer the same level of protection. The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) classifies reserves from Category Ia — strict wilderness areas with almost no human access — to Category VI — sustainable use areas where controlled resource extraction is permitted. Australia's reserve system is heavily weighted toward Category VI, largely because of the inclusion of Indigenous Protected Areas (IPAs) across the arid interior. While IPAs provide genuine protection through Indigenous land management, critics argue Australia's headline 22% protected figure overstates the rigour of its conservation network. 04 — GOVERNANCE

Who manages Australia's protected areas?

Australia's national parks are managed by a patchwork of institutions. Commonwealth, state and territory government agencies manage the majority in number, but community and joint management — which includes Indigenous co-management arrangements — covers the largest land area by contribution to the National Reserve System. Uluru-Kata Tjuta and Kakadu are among the most famous co-managed parks, where Traditional Owners hold primary custodianship. Private conservation, through nature refuges and conservation covenants, has grown significantly in recent decades as landholders choose to protect habitat on their own properties. 05 — BIOREGIONS

Which bioregions are best protected?

Australia is divided into 89 bioregions under the Interim Biogeographic Regionalisation of Australia (IBRA) framework — each a distinct ecological zone defined by its geology, climate, flora and fauna. Conservation scientists use bioregions to assess whether the full diversity of Australian ecosystems is represented in the reserve network. The results are stark: arid desert bioregions in Western Australia and the Northern Territory are well represented, often because they have little agricultural value. But biodiversity-rich regions — the tropical savannas, the temperate woodlands, the coastal heaths — remain badly underrepresented. Hover over any dot to see its exact protection level.

06 — EXTINCTION CRISIS

Australia's record of species loss

Australia has the worst mammal extinction record of any country on Earth. Since European settlement in 1788, at least 34 mammal species have been declared extinct — more than any other nation. The causes are well understood: land clearing destroyed habitat at an industrial scale, introduced predators such as foxes and cats have decimated small native mammals, and altered fire regimes have transformed entire ecosystems. The tragedy is not historical — extinctions continue today. The Bramble Cay Melomys, a small rodent living on a single island in the Torres Strait, was declared extinct in 2019 in what scientists describe as the world's first recorded mammal extinction caused by climate change. 07 — THE FULL PICTURE

Australia's protected areas at a glance

The National Reserve System is the culmination of decades of conservation effort by governments, Indigenous communities, scientists, and private landholders. It is one of the largest protected area networks on Earth by total land area. Yet the work is far from finished. Scientists estimate that at least 30% of Australia's land area needs formal protection to halt biodiversity decline — the so-called "30 by 30" target that Australia has committed to achieving by 2030.
